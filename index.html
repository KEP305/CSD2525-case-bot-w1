Worked example — different case

Before you begin, here is what this exercise looks like using a different case — an adult client with a stutter. Use it to understand the format.

The scenario

You are calling Mr. Daniel Park ahead of his first fluency evaluation. He is a 44-year-old accountant referred by his PCP for a recently worsened stutter. The chart notes onset in adolescence but no prior SLP services. He is calling on his lunch break.

Example question + rationale
"Mr. Park, I was hoping to learn a bit more about what's been going on before we meet. Can you tell me in your own words when you started noticing your speech feeling different — or harder than usual?"
Rationale: The chart says onset was in adolescence but doesn't explain what changed recently. I need to understand the timeline and what's driving the referral now — that will shape what I assess and how I prioritize the evaluation.
Example response
Mr. Park Yeah, it's — I mean, I've always had it, since I was a kid. But the last year or so it's gotten way worse. Especially at work. I do a lot of client calls and presentations, and lately I've been — I don't know, dreading them. My boss mentioned something about it and I figured I should actually do something.
Example follow-up
Follow-up "That context is really helpful, thank you. When you say it's gotten worse — are there specific situations where it's hardest, or is it most of the time now?"
Mr. Park Definitely worse in specific situations — phone calls are the worst, honestly. Face-to-face isn't as bad but it depends on who I'm talking to. One-on-one with people I know, pretty okay. Big meetings, not so much.

When you move on after the follow-up, feedback appears covering all three parts: your question, your rationale, and your follow-up.

Before you begin
Exercise instructions

It is June 30th, 2026. You are calling Mr. Reyes (Jamie's father) ahead of Jamie's first outpatient SLP evaluation. He has only a few minutes.

This is not a standard intake call. Do not ask for name, date of birth, address, or insurance — that information is already in the chart. Your goal is to fill in the largest gaps you identified from your chart review — the ones that will most directly shape how you plan and conduct this evaluation. Ask yourself: what can I not walk into that room without knowing?

For each main question, provide a brief rationale explaining what gap you are addressing and why it matters now. After each question, you may ask 1 follow-up question on that topic before moving on — or none at all. Use it when Mr. Reyes says something worth probing further.

This activity is graded for your learning but will not affect your course grade.

What feedback you will receive:

After each main question + follow-up: Feedback will cover your question (relevance to evaluation planning, wording), your rationale (clinical reasoning quality), and your follow-up if you asked one (wording). Where relevant, feedback will also address ICF contextual factors — how well your question accounted for the environmental and personal factors shaping this family's situation.

At the end: You will receive a supervisor feedback report and a case history summary — reflecting only what you asked — to download and submit to Canvas.
